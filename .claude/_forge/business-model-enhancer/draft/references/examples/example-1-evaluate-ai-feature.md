# Golden Example 1 — Evaluate branch (specific idea)

## Input
> "I run a B2B SaaS invoicing tool for freelancers, flat $15/month. I'm thinking about adding an AI feature.
> Is that a good idea, and what could go wrong?"

(Model economics are mostly present: product = invoicing SaaS, customer = freelancers, revenue = flat $15/mo
subscription. Cost driver and goal not stated.)

## A great output asserts
- **Intake:** asks only the *missing* anchors (main cost driver, current goal) — does NOT re-ask what/who/
  how-paid, which were given.
- **Anchor:** a Model snapshot naming freelancers, flat $15/mo, and their price sensitivity/churn reality.
- **Divergence:** refracts "AI feature" through ≥4 **distinct** lenses — e.g. AI as a *premium tier*
  (pricing), AI that opens *agencies/enterprise* (segment), *integrate a model vendor* (partnership),
  *metered AI usage* (business-model shift) — NOT four flavors of chatbot.
- **Independent pre-mortems:** each candidate has its own failure analysis tied to freelancer economics
  (e.g. flat-price users won't pay more; AI inference cost breaks the flat-fee margin; churn if it feels
  bolted on) — not one shared risk paragraph.
- **Scored table** present (upside · effort · risk · fit).
- **One recommendation** with an explicit "why this over the runner-up" tradeoff.
- **Winner hardened:** a devil's-advocate pre-mortem on the pick, a "what would change my mind" line, and a
  cheapest first-test (e.g. concierge AI for 10 users, or a price-probe on a paid add-on).

## A bad output (fails)
- "AI is hot, everyone's adding it — go for it."
- Four near-identical chatbot ideas.
- One generic "risks: cost, adoption, competition" note shared across all.
- A list of ideas with no recommendation, or fabricated ARR/margin numbers.

## Why
This is the core evaluate path: it proves the skill turns one idea into distinct, anchored, independently
stress-tested bets and still commits to a decision.
