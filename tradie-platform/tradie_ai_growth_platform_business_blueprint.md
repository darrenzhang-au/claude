# 1. Executive Summary

## 1.1 The business idea

Build a B2B SaaS platform that acts as an **AI growth operator for trade contractors**.

The product helps trade-business owners generate, recover and convert more work while they remain focused on delivering jobs. It does this by combining lead capture, rapid response, quote follow-up, customer reactivation, capacity-based campaigns, commercial prospecting and sales prioritisation in one trade-specific revenue system.

The central customer problem is simple:

> Tradies are often capable of performing the work, but the owner is too busy on the tools to consistently generate leads, respond quickly, follow up quotes, reactivate previous customers and maintain a healthy sales pipeline.

Most trade software is designed to manage work **after it has been won**. This product is designed to help businesses **win the work in the first place**, and to stop existing opportunities from being wasted.

## 1.2 Core promise

> **Keep your crews busy and your pipeline moving without spending your nights chasing work.**

Alternative positioning statements:

- **Your AI revenue team for the trades.**
- **More jobs. Faster follow-up. Less office work.**
- **The growth system for tradies who are stuck on the tools.**
- **Turn enquiries, old customers and open quotes into booked work.**
- **We help trade contractors win the work.**

## 1.3 Initial product wedge

The first product should not attempt to become an all-in-one CRM, job-management platform, marketplace, tender portal and AI receptionist at launch.

The strongest initial wedge is:

> **AI quote follow-up and customer reactivation for established trade contractors.**

The first version should connect to the systems contractors already use, ingest customers and quotes, identify revenue at risk, automate appropriate follow-ups and tell the owner or sales team exactly who requires human attention.

This is a strong starting point because:

1. The customer has already paid to generate the lead.
2. The quote already exists.
3. The lost revenue can be measured.
4. The workflow is frequent and repeatable.
5. The platform can demonstrate a direct financial return.
6. It avoids depending on uncertain lead-list quality at the beginning.
7. It complements established job-management software instead of immediately replacing it.

## 1.4 Long-term vision

The long-term product becomes the **revenue operating system for field-service and trade businesses**.

It continually answers:

- Where will our next jobs come from?
- Which leads should we respond to first?
- Which quotes are likely to close?
- Which opportunities are going cold?
- Which previous customers are due for another service?
- Which crews have future capacity that must be filled?
- Which commercial organisations should we approach?
- Which tenders are worth bidding for?
- What should the owner or salesperson do today?
- How much revenue is likely to be won in the next 30, 60 and 90 days?

The product should eventually behave like a combined sales coordinator, CRM administrator, customer reactivation specialist, lead-development representative and commercial opportunity analyst.

# 2. Strategic Separation From Adept Suite

## 2.1 This must be a separate application

This concept should be deliberately separated from Adept Suite.

Adept Suite is an operational platform for delivering and controlling service work. Its functional centre of gravity includes workforce operations, clock-ins, inspections, cleaner checklists, issue management, training and service delivery.

The new company would have a different primary mission:

> **Adept Suite helps service businesses deliver the work. The new platform helps trade businesses win the work.**

Although both products serve service businesses, the buyer motivation, product workflow, pricing basis, market language and competitive landscape are different.

## 2.2 Reasons to keep the businesses separate

### Clearer positioning

A product promising revenue generation should not be diluted by a broader operational message. A trade contractor needs to understand the value in seconds.

### Different buyer psychology

Adept Suite is purchased to improve operational control, compliance, workforce management and service quality. The new product is purchased to increase booked revenue and crew utilisation.

### Different sales demonstration

The Adept Suite demonstration focuses on operational visibility. The new product demonstration should focus on money currently being lost and the actions available to recover it.

### Different integration strategy

The new product should initially integrate with job-management and accounting platforms rather than force customers to replace them.

### Different long-term expansion

The new business could expand internationally across many trades without inheriting the cleaning-industry perception that may initially surround Adept Suite.

### Cleaner valuation narrative

Investors and strategic partners can evaluate the company as a horizontal AI revenue platform for the trades, rather than as an add-on inside a cleaning-operations product.

## 2.3 What can still be shared internally

The businesses may share founder knowledge, product-development experience, administrative resources, security standards and potentially selected technical patterns. However, customer data, brand, contracts, product roadmaps and market positioning should remain separate.

# 3. The Market Problem

## 3.1 The owner is the bottleneck

Many trade businesses are founded by technicians rather than professional sales operators. As the company grows, the owner commonly remains responsible for a large combination of:

- Site work
- Quoting
- Customer calls
- Scheduling
- Supplier coordination
- Staff questions
- Quality issues
- Invoicing
- Recruitment
- Business development
- Quote follow-up

Revenue generation becomes inconsistent because it competes with urgent operational work.

A leaking pipe, broken air-conditioning system, missed employee or delayed project receives immediate attention. Following up a seven-day-old quote does not. Yet the missed follow-up may represent tens of thousands of dollars in lost future work.

## 3.2 Common revenue leaks

### Slow enquiry response

New leads may wait hours or days for a response because the owner is onsite. By the time contact is made, the customer may have engaged a faster competitor.

### Missed calls

Calls frequently arrive while the contractor is driving, working, speaking with another customer or operating equipment. Many callers do not leave a detailed voicemail.

### Quotes sent without a follow-up process

Quotes are often sent and then left unattended. Owners may avoid following up because they are busy, forget, feel uncomfortable selling or assume the customer will make contact when ready.

### Weak qualification

Time is wasted attending low-value, unsuitable or geographically impractical jobs because the right questions were not asked before booking.

### Previous customers are forgotten

The business may have years of historical customers but no system for detecting repeat-service opportunities.

### Calendar capacity is reactive

Crews can become underbooked because nobody identifies capacity gaps early enough to run a targeted campaign.

### Commercial business development is inconsistent

Commercial prospecting often depends on the owner remembering to call property managers, builders or facilities contacts between jobs.

### Poor pipeline visibility

The owner cannot confidently answer how much open work exists, why jobs are being lost, which source creates the best customers or what revenue is likely next month.

### Sales administration happens after hours

The business owner performs administrative and sales work at night, leading to exhaustion, poor family balance and delayed responses.

## 3.3 Why generic CRM software often fails for this market

Traditional CRMs assume the user is sitting at a computer and actively managing a sales process. Many trade-business owners do not want to build pipelines, configure automations, maintain fields or learn enterprise sales terminology.

A trade-specific system must be:

- Mobile-first
- Highly automated
- Opinionated rather than infinitely configurable
- Fast to deploy
- Integrated with existing job and accounting data
- Based on practical actions, not CRM administration
- Able to explain commercial information in plain English
- Useful even when the owner opens it for only a few minutes each day

The customer does not want a better database. The customer wants more profitable work booked into the calendar.

# 4. Market Context and Opportunity

## 4.1 Industry backdrop

Australia has a large and fragmented construction and trade-services economy. The Australian Bureau of Statistics continues to report substantial construction activity, while industry data shows significant residential, non-residential and civil construction expenditure. The market contains a very large population of small and medium contractors, specialist subcontractors and field-service companies.

The structural opportunity is not limited to new construction. It also includes recurring maintenance, compliance services, repairs, replacement work, facilities contracts and asset servicing.

This creates a broad customer base spanning:

- Electrical
- Plumbing
- HVAC and refrigeration
- Fire protection
- Security and access control
- Solar and energy
- Roofing
- Landscaping
- Pest control
- Pool services
- Building maintenance
- Commercial cleaning
- Test and tag
- Painting
- Carpentry
- Concreting
- Glazing
- Garage doors
- Locksmiths
- Appliance repair
- Mechanical services

## 4.2 Why this market is attractive

### Fragmented customer base

No single trade company dominates the market. There are many potential SaaS customers.

### High value of incremental work

One recovered commercial quote or recurring maintenance agreement can justify months of subscription fees.

### Repetitive workflows

Most trade businesses repeatedly process enquiries, inspections, quotes, approvals, scheduling and service reminders.

### Strong need for automation

The owner frequently lacks the time or internal team required to execute disciplined sales operations.

### Existing software adoption

Many businesses already use job-management, accounting, email and scheduling systems. That means integrations can unlock usable data without requiring a complete digital transformation.

### Global expansion potential

The underlying problem is common across Australia, New Zealand, the United Kingdom, Canada and the United States.

## 4.3 Market entry should be narrow

“Tradies” is too broad for an initial go-to-market strategy. Each trade has different job values, urgency, repeat cycles, sales processes and vocabulary.

The company should begin with one or two segments where:

- The average quote value is meaningful.
- Businesses submit enough quotes to create measurable leakage.
- Repeat or recurring services exist.
- The owner has at least a small team.
- The customer can afford a premium subscription.
- Existing systems contain accessible quote and customer data.

# 5. Ideal Customer Profile

## 5.1 Primary initial ICP

The preferred initial customer is an established Australian trade contractor with:

- 3–30 field employees
- Approximately AUD $750,000 to $15 million in annual revenue
- An owner still involved in quoting or operations
- At least 20 meaningful quotes or estimates per month
- One administrator, coordinator or estimator, or an owner performing those duties
- Existing digital quoting or job-management software
- A mix of inbound leads, referrals, repeat customers and commercial opportunities
- A desire to grow without immediately hiring a full sales team

## 5.2 Best starting verticals

### HVAC and air-conditioning

Strong fit because of seasonal demand, service intervals, maintenance plans, replacement opportunities, commercial customers and relatively high quote values.

### Commercial electrical contractors

Strong fit because of project work, maintenance contracts, compliance requirements, commercial prospecting and recurring customer relationships.

### Plumbing businesses with multiple crews

Strong fit due to high enquiry volume, emergency work, quote follow-up, maintenance opportunities and capacity management.

### Fire-protection contractors

Strong fit because servicing is recurring, compliance-driven and suitable for renewal automation.

### Security and access-control contractors

Strong fit because projects can be valuable and customers often require ongoing servicing or upgrades.

### Roofing contractors

Strong fit because quote values are high, follow-up matters and leads may require strong qualification.

### Commercial landscaping and facilities-maintenance contractors

Strong fit because the business can pursue recurring contracts and reactivate existing commercial accounts.

## 5.3 Customers to avoid initially

- Newly created sole traders with very low revenue
- Operators who receive only a few jobs per month
- Businesses that refuse to use digital systems
- Primarily cash-based operators
- Customers seeking guaranteed leads without participating in the sales process
- Extremely low-ticket trades where the subscription cannot be economically justified
- Large enterprises requiring complex procurement and custom implementation before product maturity

## 5.4 Core personas

### Owner-operator growing into a company

Feels overwhelmed, quotes after hours and does not follow up consistently. Wants growth but does not want to become a full-time salesperson.

### General manager or operations manager

Responsible for keeping crews productive but lacks a reliable view of future demand.

### Office manager or service coordinator

Handles enquiries and scheduling but needs better automation, prioritisation and follow-up processes.

### Estimator or sales representative

Needs a clean pipeline, reminders, customer context and visibility into which opportunities deserve attention.

### Commercial business-development manager

Needs target accounts, contact intelligence, tender awareness, structured outreach and reusable capability information.

# 7. Product Positioning

## 7.1 Category definition

The company should attempt to define a category rather than present itself as a generic CRM.

Recommended category:

> **AI Revenue Operating System for the Trades**

Other usable category language:

- AI Growth Platform for Trade Contractors
- Trade Revenue Automation Platform
- AI Sales Coordinator for Field-Service Businesses
- Trade Pipeline and Revenue Recovery Platform

## 7.2 Positioning statement

> For established trade and field-service contractors that are too busy delivering jobs to consistently manage sales, the platform is an AI revenue operating system that responds to leads, follows up quotes, reactivates customers and fills future capacity. Unlike generic CRMs and job-management tools, it is designed around the real revenue workflows of trade businesses and tells the team what to do next without creating more administration.

## 7.3 What the platform is not

At launch, it is not:

- A complete job-management replacement
- A bookkeeping platform
- A public consumer marketplace
- A source of random low-quality leads
- A promise of guaranteed revenue
- A generic marketing agency
- A website builder
- A fully autonomous salesperson allowed to make uncontrolled commercial commitments

## 7.4 Competitive framing

The product should sit alongside systems such as ServiceM8, simPRO, AroFlo, Tradify, Fergus, Jobber, Buildxact, Xero, MYOB, Gmail and Microsoft 365.

A simple framing model:

| Business stage | Primary system category |
|---|---|
| Generate and capture demand | New platform |
| Qualify and convert opportunities | New platform |
| Quote, schedule and dispatch work | Job-management system |
| Complete and document work | Job-management/field-service system |
| Invoice and account | Accounting system |
| Reactivate and expand accounts | New platform |

The strategic opportunity is to become the intelligence and automation layer that connects these stages.

# 8. Product Principles

## 8.1 Revenue outcomes before feature volume

Every major feature should tie to one of four outcomes:

1. More qualified opportunities
2. Higher quote conversion
3. More repeat revenue
4. Better capacity utilisation

## 8.2 Action-oriented, not database-oriented

The home screen should not simply display records. It should tell the user:

- Who to call
- Which quote is at risk
- Which customer is due
- Which campaign is recommended
- Where capacity is emerging
- What revenue may be recovered

## 8.3 Human approval at consequential moments

AI may draft, classify, summarise and recommend. The user should initially approve actions that could materially affect price, scope, legal commitments, customer relationships or brand reputation.

## 8.4 Integrate before replacing

The platform should pull data from systems customers already use. Replacement of job-management functionality should not be an early objective.

## 8.5 Mobile-first operational design

Owners should be able to review and act from a phone between jobs.

## 8.6 Plain trade language

Use terms such as enquiries, jobs, quotes, customers, crews, service area and follow-up. Avoid requiring users to understand complex CRM language.

## 8.7 Measurable ROI

The product should attribute recovered or influenced revenue wherever reasonably possible.

# 9. Product Architecture by Capability

## 9.1 Revenue Command Centre

The home screen provides a daily commercial briefing.

Example:

- 8 new enquiries require review
- 4 quotes worth $37,500 are going cold
- 2 high-intent customers should be called today
- 31 previous customers are due for service
- Crew 2 has 1.5 unfilled days next week
- $84,000 is currently in the open quote pipeline
- $16,200 was recovered through automated follow-up this month

The command centre should include:

- Today’s priority actions
- Pipeline value
- Weighted forecast
- Quote ageing
- Response speed
- Conversion rate
- Reactivation opportunities
- Future capacity alerts
- Campaign performance
- Revenue influenced by the platform

## 9.2 Lead Capture and Unified Enquiry Inbox

Capture enquiries from:

- Website forms
- Email
- Phone and missed calls
- SMS
- Facebook and Instagram leads
- Google Business Profile pathways where permitted
- Referral forms
- Imported leads
- Manual entry
- Third-party marketplaces

The unified inbox should:

- Identify the customer
- Detect duplicates
- Classify the service requested
- Determine urgency
- Capture job location
- Request photographs or documents
- Estimate likely job value range
- Assign the opportunity
- Recommend a next step
- Track response time

## 9.3 AI Enquiry Qualification

The system asks trade-specific questions.

Examples for HVAC:

- Is the request residential or commercial?
- Is the system operating at all?
- What brand and model is installed?
- How many units are affected?
- Is access available to the roof or plant area?
- Are photographs available?
- Is this repair, replacement or scheduled servicing?

Examples for roofing:

- Is the issue active leaking, restoration or replacement?
- What roof type is present?
- How many storeys?
- Is there safe access?
- Is an insurance claim involved?
- Are drone or roof photographs available?

Qualification should reduce unnecessary site visits and route urgent or high-value work appropriately.

## 9.4 Missed-Call Recovery

When the business misses a call, the system may send a configurable SMS:

> Thanks for calling [Business]. We are currently onsite. What work do you need help with, and what suburb is the property in?

The AI can continue structured intake and offer appropriate next steps. Customers must always be able to reach a person or request a callback.

## 9.5 Quote and Opportunity Pipeline

Quotes can enter through direct integration, email parsing, API, CSV import or manual entry.

Each opportunity includes:

- Customer
- Service type
- Quote amount
- Gross-margin estimate where available
- Date issued
- Expiry date
- Assigned owner
- Probability
- Engagement history
- Last interaction
- Next action
- Objections
- Competitors mentioned
- Expected decision date
- Loss reason
- Source
- Job-management record link

Suggested stages:

1. New enquiry
2. Qualifying
3. Site visit or assessment booked
4. Quote being prepared
5. Quote sent
6. Follow-up active
7. Verbal approval
8. Won
9. Lost
10. Deferred

## 9.6 AI Quote Follow-Up Engine

This is the initial flagship feature.

The system should select follow-up timing and messaging based on:

- Quote value
- Customer type
- Residential or commercial context
- Service urgency
- Quote age
- Customer engagement
- Existing relationship
- Known decision date
- Whether the quote has been viewed
- Previous responses
- Salesperson activity
- Trade-specific buying cycle

Example sequence:

### Immediately after sending

Confirm receipt and explain the next step.

### Two business days later

Ask whether the customer has questions and offer clarification.

### Five business days later

Share a relevant proof point, project example, warranty or availability update.

### Seven to ten business days later

Prompt a personal call for high-value opportunities or send a respectful decision check-in.

### Later

Move the quote into a nurture or future-review sequence rather than repeatedly contacting the customer.

The engine must prevent inappropriate communication. It should pause when:

- The customer responds
- The salesperson manually contacts the customer
- The quote is approved or declined
- The customer opts out
- A complaint or sensitive issue is detected
- The account requires manual handling

## 9.7 Engagement and Buying-Intent Signals

Potential signals include:

- Email opened repeatedly
- Quote viewed multiple times
- Quote forwarded or shared, where technically and legally available
- Pricing page viewed
- Customer replied with questions
- Customer requested insurance or licence documents
- Decision date approaching
- Related stakeholder added
- Service urgency increased

The system should translate signals into a simple recommendation:

> **Call today:** The customer viewed the quote three times and opened the warranty document.

## 9.8 Customer Reactivation Engine

The system analyses customer history to identify likely future work.

Opportunity types include:

- Recurring servicing
- Warranty expiry
- Preventative maintenance
- Seasonal service
- Compliance renewal
- Previous quote not proceeding
- Deferred work
- Equipment age
- Multi-site expansion
- Previous emergency repair suggesting replacement
- Uncompleted recommendations from an earlier job

The system should create small, targeted campaigns rather than send generic bulk messages.

Example:

> 42 commercial customers had air-conditioning maintenance between 10 and 14 months ago. A service reminder campaign could generate approximately 12–18 bookings based on your historical conversion rate.

## 9.9 Capacity Intelligence and Empty-Calendar Recovery

This capability connects future crew availability with demand generation.

The system identifies:

- Unfilled crew days
- Cancelled jobs
- Low utilisation by geography
- Seasonal slow periods
- Underused service lines
- Employees with relevant skills available

It then recommends actions:

- Contact customers due for service
- Offer earlier appointments to warm leads
- Reactivate deferred quotes
- Promote a relevant seasonal service
- Contact commercial accounts in the affected area
- Consolidate nearby jobs
- Open limited appointment inventory to selected customers

The objective is not indiscriminate discounting. The objective is to generate the right type of work for the available capacity.

## 9.10 Commercial Account Prospecting

After the core quote and reactivation product is proven, introduce account-based prospecting.

The business defines:

- Target industries
- Target property types
- Geographic coverage
- Minimum account size
- Services offered
- Certifications and capabilities
- Preferred contract type

The platform identifies suitable organisations such as:

- Property managers
- Strata managers
- Builders
- Facilities-management firms
- Retail groups
- Childcare operators
- Schools
- Medical centres
- Warehouses
- Manufacturers
- Hotels
- Aged-care providers
- Body corporates
- Real-estate groups

It should prioritise relevance and commercial fit rather than simply producing thousands of contacts.

## 9.11 AI Outreach Assistant

The platform prepares personalised outreach based on a target account’s likely needs and the contractor’s actual capabilities.

Functions include:

- Draft email sequences
- Draft LinkedIn or call talking points where appropriate
- Recommend the right contact role
- Create account research summaries
- Suggest a relevant offer
- Detect replies
- Classify interest
- Book meetings
- Stop sequences when a person responds
- Create human call tasks

Safeguards must address spam compliance, consent, reputation and sender-domain health.

## 9.12 Tender and Opportunity Intelligence

Long-term capabilities may include:

- Tender monitoring
- Opportunity matching
- Bid/no-bid scoring
- Deadline tracking
- Required-document checklists
- Capability library
- Reusable response content
- Compliance-document expiry alerts
- Draft response assistance
- Reference-project matching
- Submission approvals
- Debrief and win/loss analysis

This should be treated as a later module because tender workflows can become complex and specialised. The market already includes dedicated tender-discovery and bid-management products.

## 9.13 Reviews and Referrals

After successful completion, the system can:

- Request a review
- Direct dissatisfied customers to private recovery rather than a public review request
- Ask for a referral
- Create referral offers
- Track referral sources
- Request permission to use project photographs or testimonials

## 9.14 AI Sales Coach

The product can coach the owner or staff with practical observations:

- Quotes sent within 24 hours convert better than slower quotes.
- Commercial maintenance quotes are being followed up too late.
- Roofing leads from a particular source have low margin.
- One estimator has strong close rates but slow quote preparation.
- Customers frequently object to unclear exclusions.
- Quotes above a threshold close more often after a personal call.

Recommendations must be grounded in the customer’s own data and clearly distinguish correlation from proven causation.

## 9.15 Revenue Forecasting

The forecast combines:

- Open pipeline
- Historical conversion by stage
- Quote age
- Customer engagement
- Salesperson performance
- Seasonality
- Expected decision dates
- Capacity constraints
- Recurring service schedules

Outputs:

- Expected 30-day bookings
- Expected 60-day bookings
- Expected 90-day bookings
- Upside scenario
- At-risk revenue
- Capacity mismatch

# 11. User Experience

## 11.1 Daily home experience

The user should open the application and immediately see a prioritised list.

Example:

### Today

1. Call Morrison Property Group — $18,400 quote, strong engagement
2. Approve follow-up to Liam — quote expires Friday
3. Review 3 new enquiries collected overnight
4. Launch annual service reminder to 14 eligible customers
5. Crew capacity alert — two open days next week

## 11.2 Mobile quick actions

- Call customer
- Send approved message
- Record outcome by voice
- Mark won or lost
- Snooze until a date
- Assign to staff
- Open source quote
- Ask AI for a summary

## 11.3 Voice-first input

Because users may be driving or onsite, the system should support voice notes such as:

> Spoke to John. They are waiting for the landlord’s approval. Follow up next Thursday. Main concern is the switchboard upgrade cost.

The platform converts this into structured notes, objection data and a dated follow-up task.

## 11.4 Simple onboarding

A strong onboarding process should:

1. Connect the customer’s job-management system.
2. Connect email.
3. Import open quotes and customer history.
4. Ask the user to define services, service area and ideal jobs.
5. Review communication tone.
6. Choose approval or automatic modes.
7. Show immediate revenue opportunities.

The first “aha” moment should occur quickly:

> You currently have $126,000 in open quotes. $38,000 has not received follow-up in more than seven days.

# 12. AI System Design

## 12.1 AI roles

The platform may use specialised AI agents or services for:

- Enquiry classification
- Qualification conversation
- Email and SMS drafting
- Quote-risk scoring
- Buying-intent summarisation
- Customer reactivation matching
- Capacity campaign recommendations
- Account research
- Reply classification
- Call and voice-note summarisation
- Tender matching
- Forecast explanation

## 12.2 AI should not control pricing without safeguards

Pricing, discounts, contractual promises and scope commitments are high-risk areas. Early versions should:

- Draft rather than autonomously approve quotes
- Use authorised price books only
- Require approval for discounts
- Clearly show assumptions
- Keep an audit trail
- Escalate uncertain requests

## 12.3 Confidence and explainability

Recommendations should state why they were made.

Poor:

> This lead has a score of 87.

Better:

> High priority because the customer replied yesterday, viewed the quote again this morning and wants the work completed this month.

## 12.4 Customer-specific learning

The system should learn from:

- Won and lost work
- Follow-up timing
- Customer segments
- Quote values
- Staff activity
- Service categories
- Objections
- Seasonality

The system must avoid silently learning harmful or unlawful patterns.

## 12.5 AI safety model

Communication modes:

1. **Draft only** — AI prepares content, human sends.
2. **Approval queue** — AI schedules content, human approves batches.
3. **Guarded automation** — approved templates and rules send automatically.
4. **Advanced autonomy** — only after sufficient trust, controls and evidence.

# 15. Go-to-Market Strategy

## 15.1 Founder-led market entry

The founder should initially sell directly to learn:

- Which revenue leak hurts most
- What language resonates
- Which integrations are essential
- What level of automation customers trust
- How customers calculate ROI
- Why customers churn or fail to implement

## 15.2 Initial offer

Do not begin by selling a broad “AI platform.” Sell a concrete financial outcome.

Recommended initial offer:

> **We identify the quotes and previous customers currently being neglected, then install an automated follow-up system that helps convert them into booked work.**

## 15.3 Revenue leak audit

A powerful sales tool is a no-obligation or low-cost **Revenue Leak Audit**.

The audit analyses:

- Number and value of open quotes
- Quotes with no recent follow-up
- Average response time
- Old customers eligible for reactivation
- Lost quote reasons
- Pipeline coverage
- Potential recurring-service opportunities

Output example:

> In the last 12 months, the business issued $2.1 million in quotes. $610,000 received no recorded follow-up. A 5% recovery would represent $30,500 in additional work.

The audit becomes both a sales method and an onboarding mechanism.

## 15.4 Pilot design

Run pilots with approximately 5–10 carefully selected businesses in one vertical.

Pilot requirements:

- Sufficient quote volume
- Historical data access
- Willingness to use the workflow
- Weekly feedback
- Agreement on baseline metrics
- Permission to develop an anonymised case study if successful

### Job-management consultants

Consultants implementing ServiceM8, simPRO, AroFlo and similar products may become referral partners.

### Marketing agencies serving tradies

Agencies generate leads but are frequently blamed when the contractor fails to respond. The product can improve lead conversion and protect agency performance.

### Equipment suppliers and wholesalers

Potential strategic channel because they already serve large contractor networks.

### Educational content

Content themes:

- How much revenue sits in unfollowed quotes

- How to reactivate old customers
- How to fill a slow month
- When a trade business should hire a salesperson

The best demonstration uses the prospect’s own data or a realistic industry example.

Demonstration sequence:

1. Show neglected open quote value.
2. Show the AI-generated priority queue.
3. Show a safe follow-up sequence.

# 17. Competitive Landscape

## 17.1 Job-management platforms

Platforms such as ServiceM8, Jobber, Tradify, Fergus, simPRO and AroFlo already provide combinations of quoting, scheduling, job management, invoicing and customer records. Some are adding AI-assisted quoting, writing and scheduling features.

The new product should not assume these platforms are static. They may expand further into lead and sales automation.

## 17.2 Generic CRMs

HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive, Zoho, GoHighLevel and other CRMs can technically support many sales workflows. Their weaknesses for this segment may include setup complexity, generic terminology, weak job-capacity context and a need for ongoing administration.

## 17.3 Marketing automation and AI agencies

Many agencies build missed-call text-back, chatbots, email sequences and lead-nurture systems using generic tools. They validate demand but often lack deep productisation, integrated revenue data, capacity intelligence and trade-specific analytics.

## 17.4 Tender platforms

Tender-discovery and bid-management tools already exist. Tender functionality should not be treated as uncontested territory.

## 17.5 Potential moat

The moat will not be “we use AI.” AI functionality will become widely available.

Potential defensibility comes from:

- Deep integrations with trade systems
- Proprietary structured revenue and workflow data
- Trade-specific playbooks
- Customer-specific performance learning
- Capacity-to-demand intelligence
- High-quality outcome attribution
- Strong distribution partnerships
- Trusted automation and compliance controls
- Embedded daily workflow
- Benchmarks across anonymised industry cohorts, subject to privacy and legal controls

# 18. Differentiation Strategy

## 18.1 Do not sell leads; build a revenue machine

Lead marketplaces often produce competition, disputes over lead quality and customer distrust. The platform should first help customers maximise assets they already own:

- Existing enquiries
- Existing quotes
- Existing customers
- Existing capacity
- Existing relationships

## 18.2 Join sales data to operational capacity

This is a meaningful differentiator.

A generic CRM knows the value of the pipeline. A trade revenue platform should know whether the business has the crews, skills, geography and time to deliver the work.

## 18.3 Recommend the next best commercial action

The product should not make users design complex workflows. It should deliver a daily prioritised action list.

## 18.4 Learn trade-specific revenue patterns

Examples:

- Emergency plumbing needs immediate response.
- Commercial HVAC replacement may have multiple decision-makers.
- Roofing work may be affected by weather and insurance.
- Fire servicing follows compliance cycles.
- Solar sales may involve financing and longer nurture.

## 18.5 Demonstrate recovered revenue

The customer must be able to see what changed after implementation.

## 19.4 Indicative value calculation

Example customer:

- 40 quotes per month
- Average quote value: $5,000
- Monthly quoted value: $200,000
- Current win rate: 25%
- Improvement after stronger follow-up: 3 percentage points

Incremental booked revenue:

- $200,000 × 3% = $6,000 per month

At a 35% gross margin:

- $2,100 incremental gross profit per month

A $499–$999 monthly subscription may be economically rational if the result is credible and sustained.

This is an example only; the company must validate actual outcomes and avoid presenting unsupported promises.

# 24. Key Risks and Mitigations

## 24.1 Existing platforms add the same features

**Risk:** Job-management providers extend into AI follow-up and reactivation.

**Mitigation:** Move quickly, integrate broadly, specialise in revenue intelligence and build cross-platform value that a single job-management provider may not prioritise.

## 24.2 Poor data quality

**Risk:** Customer records, quote statuses and service categories are incomplete.

**Mitigation:** Build onboarding diagnostics, data-cleaning workflows and confidence labels.

## 24.3 Customer blames software for poor lead quality

**Risk:** The product becomes associated with generic lead-generation promises.

**Mitigation:** Begin with existing quotes and customers, where source and value are clearer.

## 24.4 Automation damages customer relationships

**Risk:** Messages are repetitive, poorly timed or inappropriate.

**Mitigation:** Approval modes, pause-on-reply, frequency controls, account exclusions and audit trails.

## 24.5 Attribution disputes

**Risk:** It is unclear whether the platform caused a sale.

**Mitigation:** Use transparent attribution categories such as sourced, recovered, assisted and unverified.

## 24.6 Tradies resist another system

**Risk:** Users do not maintain CRM data.

**Mitigation:** Synchronise existing systems, automate data capture and focus the interface on actions rather than administration.

## 24.7 The platform becomes too broad

**Risk:** Development spreads across CRM, job management, marketing, AI calling, tenders and marketplace functions.

**Mitigation:** Enforce a wedge strategy and require measurable evidence before expanding.

## 24.8 AI cost and margin pressure

**Risk:** Heavy AI, voice or enrichment usage reduces gross margin.

**Mitigation:** Use appropriate models, caching, deterministic workflows, usage limits and tiered pricing.

## 24.9 Deliverability risk

**Risk:** Email outreach damages sender reputation.

**Mitigation:** Domain-health controls, conservative sending, authentication requirements, compliance checks and focus on relevant small-batch outreach.

# 30. Final Business Thesis

The opportunity is not to create another generic tradie CRM or another marketplace selling shared leads.

The opportunity is to create a focused revenue system for established trade contractors that are operationally capable but commercially inconsistent because the owner and management team are consumed by delivery.

The initial product should recover value already present in the business:

- Open quotes
- Unanswered enquiries
- Previous customers
- Deferred work
- Recurring service cycles
- Future crew capacity

Once the platform earns trust and proves ROI, it can expand into:

- Lead qualification
- Commercial prospecting
- Account-based outreach
- Capacity generation
- Tender intelligence
- Revenue forecasting

The strongest strategic position is:

> **Job-management software helps contractors organise the work they have won. This platform helps them create, recover and convert the work they need next.**

The product wins when a contractor no longer depends on memory, spare time or late-night administration to keep the pipeline moving.
